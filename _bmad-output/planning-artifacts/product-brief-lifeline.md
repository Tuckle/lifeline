# Product Brief: Lifeline

## Executive Summary

Lifeline is a private, visual timeline for understanding a human life. It gives people a single vertical line where their past scrolls upward, their future extends downward, and the important moments of their life can be captured, enriched, revisited, and reflected on over time. Users can add dated memories, photos, stories, notes, and major events, while also importing digital traces from RescueTime, social media, Google Keep, Notion, and similar sources.

The product is built for therapy, self-reflection, and personal growth users: people who want more than a journal and more than passive activity tracking. They want to understand their patterns, remember what shaped them, see where they did well, make sense of difficult periods, and hold a clearer hope for the future. Lifeline turns fragmented personal data into a meaningful autobiographical interface.

The opportunity is to create the "life graph" for the individual: not a feed, not a productivity dashboard, and not a diary archive, but a private system of memory, reflection, and perspective. Existing journaling tools capture entries; quantified-self tools capture behavior; social apps capture performance for others. Lifeline combines authored memories and imported evidence into one beautiful, weighted life timeline that helps users see the arc of their own becoming.

## The Problem

People produce an enormous amount of personal history, but it is scattered across incompatible surfaces. Meaningful moments live in photo libraries, social posts, notes apps, therapy reflections, productivity tools, chat logs, screenshots, and memory. Daily behavior data lives in systems like RescueTime, but rarely connects to the stories that explain what was happening in a person's life.

The result is a fragmented sense of self. Someone may know that a year was hard, or that they grew a lot, but they cannot easily see the relationship between events, habits, environments, and decisions. Journals can become chronological piles of text. Social media is public-facing and emotionally distorted. Productivity analytics show activity, but not meaning. Therapy notes and personal reflections are useful in the moment, but often disappear into private documents and forgotten notebooks.

For growth-oriented users, this fragmentation has a real cost. They lose continuity. They forget lessons. They struggle to recognize patterns until they repeat. They cannot easily revisit evidence of resilience, progress, relationships, turning points, or unfinished hopes. They need a way to see their life as a navigable whole.

## The Solution

Lifeline creates a vertical infinite timeline of a user's life. Up is history; down is the future. Every memory, event, reflection, photo, note, imported activity period, and referenced social post can attach to a date or date range and appear in its proper place. Users can mark some moments as more important than others, allowing the line to visually distinguish defining life events from everyday context.

The first version should support manual life events, photos, written stories, importance weighting, and day-one imports from RescueTime and notes sources. RescueTime gives Lifeline behavioral texture: what the user was doing on their devices during a given period. Notes imports from Google Keep or Notion give it reflective texture: what the user was thinking, planning, writing, or feeling. Together, these create a richer timeline than either journaling or tracking alone.

Imported data should enter as suggested context, not as a flood of permanent life events. Lifeline should help users review imported activity blocks, notes, and social references, then promote only the meaningful pieces onto the main line. This curation step protects the product's emotional clarity: the timeline should feel like a life, not a database dump.

The core interaction is not "write every day." It is "place meaning on the line of your life." Users can add childhood memories, reference old photos, mark a breakup, a move, a career shift, a recovery period, a creative project, a low season, a breakthrough, or a future intention. Over time, Lifeline becomes a private autobiographical map that supports reflection, therapy, annual reviews, identity work, and long-term personal development.

## What Makes This Different

Most adjacent products optimize for one of three behaviors: journaling, memory resurfacing, or activity tracking. Day One and Journey-style apps help people keep dated entries with media and metadata. Timehop-style products resurface old social content. RescueTime-style products quantify attention and device usage. Lifeline's differentiator is that it treats all of these as source material for a larger purpose: understanding the pattern and meaning of a life.

The product's emotional center is personal growth rather than capture. It is private by default, designed for self-understanding before sharing. It lets users weight moments by importance, so the interface can reflect personal significance instead of treating every entry as equal. It also explicitly includes the future, making the timeline a bridge between memory and intention rather than a static archive.

The strongest early advantage is the product concept itself: a clear, emotionally resonant organizing metaphor that can absorb multiple data sources without becoming a generic data dashboard. If executed with taste, privacy, and restraint, Lifeline can feel less like software and more like a place where a person can meet their own life.

## Who This Serves

The primary user is a reflective person with personal development or therapy-adjacent goals. They may journal, go to therapy, review their year, track habits, save notes, or feel drawn to quantified-self tools, but they are not satisfied with fragmented records. They want to understand their patterns and their story.

Early users are likely to include people in life transitions: recovering from burnout, changing careers, rebuilding after a difficult period, processing childhood or family history, documenting creative growth, or trying to become more intentional. Their success moment is: "I can finally see what happened, what it meant, and where I want to go next."

Secondary users may eventually include therapists, coaches, memoir writers, families preserving personal history, and highly analytical self-trackers. These should not lead the first version, but they are useful expansion paths once the private individual experience is strong.

## MVP Scope

The MVP should create a complete first-use experience around one private life timeline:

- Create and browse a vertical infinite timeline where past appears upward and future appears downward.
- Add dated life events, memories, written reflections, and stories.
- Attach or reference photos to events.
- Mark event importance so major life moments visually stand out.
- Import RescueTime data to show what the user was doing on devices during selected periods.
- Import or reference notes from Notion and Google Keep-style sources where feasible.
- Review imported items before they become primary timeline events.
- Search, filter, and inspect events by date, importance, source, and theme.
- Add future intentions or planned milestones below the present point.
- Keep all data private by default, with clear delete, disconnect, and export controls.

The first version should avoid collaborative family timelines, public profiles, therapist portals, complex AI interpretation, and broad social publishing. AI-assisted pattern summaries may become valuable later, but the MVP should not position itself as a therapist or diagnostician. It should help users organize, revisit, and reflect on their own material.

## Success Criteria

Lifeline is working if users feel a measurable increase in self-understanding and continuity. The most important success signal is not daily streaks; it is whether users return when they want perspective on their life.

Early product metrics should include:

- Activation: percentage of users who create or import at least 10 meaningful timeline items in the first session.
- Integration value: percentage of users who connect at least one data source, especially RescueTime or notes.
- Reflection depth: percentage of users who mark importance, add narrative context, or revisit older events.
- Retention by reflection moment: users returning for weekly reviews, therapy preparation, monthly reflection, or annual review.
- Trust: low deletion/churn after import, clear privacy comprehension, and positive qualitative feedback around safety.
- Aha moment: users explicitly report understanding a life pattern, rediscovering a forgotten period, or seeing a clearer future direction.

## Market Context

The market already validates pieces of the behavior. Journaling apps provide media-rich dated entries and metadata. Timeline-first journal products show that the interface has appeal. RescueTime exposes activity and productivity data that can support behavioral analysis. Notion's API ecosystem makes personal knowledge imports plausible. However, the market is still missing a product that unifies meaningful memories, digital evidence, importance weighting, and future orientation into one private life visualization.

This is not primarily a "better journal" wedge. It is a reflective life visualization product that begins with journaling-like inputs and tracking imports. That positioning matters: it gives Lifeline permission to care about aesthetics, emotional resonance, personal privacy, and long-term meaning instead of competing only on entry capture features.

## Risks And Open Questions

The largest product risk is overwhelm. A whole-life timeline can become emotionally heavy, visually cluttered, or technically messy if imports flood the product with low-signal data. Lifeline needs strong defaults that separate "context" from "meaningful moments." RescueTime and notes imports should enrich the timeline without burying the user.

The second major risk is privacy and trust. Users will only import intimate notes, childhood memories, device activity, and social history if they believe Lifeline is private, understandable, and respectful. Privacy cannot be decorative; it must shape onboarding, data storage, source permissions, deletion flows, and export.

The third risk is unclear behavioral cadence. Lifeline should not require daily journaling to feel alive. The product needs natural use cases: therapy preparation, life review, monthly reflection, yearly review, major transition processing, and future planning.

Open questions remain around the exact first platform, the depth of Google Keep import feasibility, whether imported social posts should be copied or referenced, and how much structure users need when reflecting on patterns.

## Vision

If Lifeline succeeds, it becomes the private operating system for personal memory and growth. A person could scroll through childhood, adolescence, relationships, work eras, losses, wins, habits, creative projects, and future hopes in one continuous interface. They could see what shaped them, what keeps repeating, what they have survived, what they are building, and what they want to become.

In two to three years, Lifeline could support richer imports, guided reflection, pattern detection, therapy and coaching preparation, optional sharing of selected stories, family memory collaboration, and AI-assisted autobiographical synthesis. But the foundation should remain simple and sacred: one private line, beautifully rendered, where a person can see their life.
